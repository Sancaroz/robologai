Thread:

1/ Humanoid robots are not one market anymore.

In 2026, the race is splitting into four buyer lanes:

factory, warehouse, research, and home.

That changes how we should compare Optimus, Figure, Digit, Apollo, Unitree, NEO, and the rest.

2/ Factory humanoids are chasing structured work:

part movement, machine tending, inspection support, assembly assistance, and repetitive physical tasks.

The key signal is not a viral demo.

It is repeatability.

3/ Warehouse robots may be the closest near-term lane.

Logistics has measurable work: tote movement, loading, unloading, sorting, AMR support, and shift coverage.

The winning metric is utilization, not personality.

4/ Research robots are a different market.

A platform like Unitree G1 can matter even if it is not ready for paid labor, because it gives builders real hardware for AI, control, simulation, and motion planning.

Price visibility matters here.

5/ Home robots are the most exciting and the hardest.

Homes have clutter, stairs, pets, privacy concerns, food, furniture, kids, and emotional expectations.

A home robot needs trust before it needs magic.

6/ My buyer map:

Do not ask only "which robot is best?"

Ask:
- what job first?
- what stage?
- what price signal?
- what video proof?
- what source confidence?
- what alternatives?

Short follow-up post:

The humanoid robot market will probably not be won by the flashiest demo.

safe, repeatable, maintainable, affordable, and clear about its first job.
